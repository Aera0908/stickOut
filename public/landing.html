Understanding the Basics

What is a Stick Diagram?

A stick diagram is a simplified, abstracted representation of a VLSI (Very-Large-Scale Integration) integrated circuit layout. It maps the topology of a CMOS circuit — showing transistors, wiring, and connections — without specifying exact physical dimensions.

Stick diagrams are essential in IC design education and early-stage CMOS layout planning. They bridge the gap between a circuit schematic and a full mask layout, helping engineers visualize how transistors, metal interconnects, polysilicon gates, and diffusion regions are physically arranged on silicon.

Each layer in a stick diagram is represented by a different color:

Metal 2
Polysilicon
P-Diffusion
N-Diffusion

Frequently Asked Questions

Common Questions About Stick Diagrams

What is a stick diagram in VLSI design?

A stick diagram is a simplified representation of an integrated circuit layout that shows the relative positions of transistors, wiring (metal interconnects), polysilicon gates, and diffusion regions without specifying exact physical dimensions. Stick diagrams use color-coded lines to represent different fabrication layers — for example, blue for Metal 1, purple for Polysilicon, yellow for P-Diffusion, and green for N-Diffusion. They are a critical step between circuit schematics and full mask layouts in CMOS IC design.

What is the difference between a stick diagram and a layout diagram?

A stick diagram is an abstracted, topological representation that shows the relative placement and connectivity of circuit elements without precise dimensions. A layout diagram (or mask layout) is a geometrically accurate representation with exact widths, spacings, and coordinates that can be sent to fabrication. Stick diagrams are typically drawn first to plan the layout before creating the final mask-level design in professional EDA tools.
